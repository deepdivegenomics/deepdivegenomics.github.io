Day by day

Three days, built so each one unblocks the next.

Each day has a live teaching session followed by hands-on labs you run yourself. Sessions are recorded. Everything runs in a pre-built conda environment so you spend your time on the actual analysis, not on setup.

  • Navigating a real filesystem: paths, ls -lh, globbing, tab-completion, and where your data actually lives on a server.
  • Pipes and redirection — the single idea that makes the whole toolchain compose. Counting reads in a FASTQ with zcat | wc -l before any "real" tool.
  • grep, awk, sort, cut on tab-delimited genomics files — the daily-driver skills no tutorial teaches you.
  • Writing a bash loop over many samples, with variables, permissions, chmod +x and the shebang line.
  • Reproducible environments with conda/mamba — then your first Python: reading files, loading a table with pandas, and a quick plot.
Labs — set up your environment, write a sample-sheet parser in bash, and read your first file in Python. Outcome: at home both in a terminal and in a Python script.
  • Anatomy of a FASTQ record and Phred quality scores — reading them, not just trusting them.
  • Quality control with FastQC + MultiQC: spotting adapter contamination and over-represented sequences, then trimming with fastp.
  • Reference genomes: GRCh38 vs hg19, why the build matters, and indexing with bwa index / samtools faidx.
  • Aligning with BWA-MEM, read groups (and why every downstream tool nags you about them), and the SAM/BAM format field by field.
  • Sorting, indexing, marking duplicates, and reading a samtools flagstat report critically.
Lab — align NA12878, mark duplicates, and view your BAM in IGV. Outcome: an analysis-ready, deduplicated BAM you built yourself.
  • Base Quality Score Recalibration: the systematic error it corrects and how to tell if it actually helped.
  • GATK HaplotypeCaller and local re-assembly — the intuition for why it re-aligns around candidate sites.
  • The VCF format in depth: INFO, FORMAT, AD/DP/GQ, and what a 0/1 genotype really means.
  • Filtering calls, reading one in IGV to decide for yourself, and benchmarking against the GIAB truth set with hap.py — precision and recall for your own pipeline.
  • Annotating with Ensembl VEP, then a Python lab to load your variants into pandas, summarise them and plot depth and quality.
  • Where to go next: automating with Nextflow, joint genotyping across samples, somatic calling, and running on an HPC scheduler.
Capstone lab — take a BAM all the way to a filtered, annotated VCF and summarise it in Python. Outcome: a complete variant-calling pipeline you ran end-to-end yourself.

How it runs

Small, live, and built around the lab — not the lecture.

Each batch is limited to 30 students so the instructors can give proper attention to everyone. If more than 30 people register, additional students are moved to the next batch. Live sessions are for working through problems together; the main learning happens in the hands-on labs, with help available on WhatsApp.

  • Three live sessions, one per day, 10:00 AM – 1:30 PM IST across Fri–Sun. Always recorded.
  • Hands-on labs each afternoon you work through at your own pace, with the lab guide to keep.
  • A private WhatsApp group with the instructors and TA answering through the workshop and the week after.
  • A follow-up doubt-clearing session the week after, for anyone stuck on a lab or wanting to talk through their own data.

FAQ

Common questions

No. Day 1 starts from the very beginning — basic command line usage and just enough Python to work with variant tables. If you've never written code before, this workshop is designed for you. If you already code but are new to genomics, you'll move through day one quickly and get into the biology from day two.

Any laptop with about 20 GB of free disk space. macOS and Linux work natively; on Windows we'll get you set up with WSL2 in the first session (it's painless these days). The practice dataset is deliberately small — a chr20 subset — so you do not need a powerful machine or cluster access to complete every lab.

The germline short-variant pipeline you build follows the same workflow used in both research and diagnostic settings, so the skills apply to either. That said, this is an educational workshop — it is not accredited clinical training and should not be used for patient decisions.

During the labs, everyone uses the same well-characterised sample so we can compare calls against a known truth set. The follow-up session is a good place to bring your own data — share a BAM or a FASTQ and we'll work through it together.

Every session is recorded and shared the same day. The labs can be done at your own pace. If you miss a live session, you can catch up from the recording and ask questions in the WhatsApp group.

The information itself is freely available online. What's different here is structure, feedback, and a small batch where someone actually looks at your work. You get three focused days with instructors who can help when you're stuck — which is usually what stops self-learners from finishing.
